Schools use three different systems to support students who are struggling. Each has a different legal foundation, a different level of formality, and a different set of services attached. Families often hear all three terms in the same conversation without knowing which applies to their child. Here is a plain-language map.

## MTSS (Multi-Tiered System of Supports)

MTSS is the school's general-education response to students who are not yet identified with a disability but are struggling. It is not a disability category. It is a process.

MTSS organizes support in three tiers:

- **Tier 1:** general classroom instruction for all students.
- **Tier 2:** small-group intervention for students who need more than Tier 1 alone. Usually 30-minute pull-out groups, two or three times per week.
- **Tier 3:** intensive one-on-one or very small-group intervention for students who have not responded to Tier 2.

If your child is in Tier 2 or Tier 3 and is not making progress, the school has a federal obligation under the Every Student Succeeds Act and IDEA Early Intervening Services to intensify the support or refer the child for a special education evaluation. MTSS is not a holding pattern. If your child has been in Tier 2 for more than a semester with no measurable progress, the system is failing your child, not waiting on your child.

## Section 504

A 504 plan, named for Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, provides accommodations for a student with a disability who does not need specialized instruction.

A child qualifies for a 504 plan if a documented physical or mental impairment substantially limits a major life activity. The disability is real and recognized. The student does not need a specially designed curriculum. They need adjustments to access the general curriculum.

Common 504 accommodations include extended time on tests, preferential seating, permission to take breaks, written instructions in addition to spoken ones, and access to assistive technology.

Compared to an IEP, a 504 plan is faster to obtain, less rigorous in its monitoring requirements, and provides no specialized instruction. It is the right tool when a child needs access adjustments. It is the wrong tool when a child needs the curriculum itself adapted.

## IEP

An Individualized Education Program, named for the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, is the most comprehensive of the three. It applies to students with one of 13 federally recognized disability categories whose disability adversely affects educational performance and who, because of the disability, need specially designed instruction.

An IEP includes:

- Measurable annual goals
- Specialized instruction tailored to the child's needs
- Related services such as speech therapy, occupational therapy, counseling, or transportation
- Specific accommodations and modifications
- A statement of placement in the least restrictive environment
- Procedural safeguards that protect the family's rights at every step

An IEP is the right tool when a child needs the curriculum, the instruction, or the school environment changed to meet their needs.

## How to know which one your child needs

A quick framework:

- **Your child is struggling but does not have a diagnosis or formal disability designation.** Start with MTSS. Ask the school in writing for Tier 2 or Tier 3 intervention and a clear timeline for progress monitoring.
- **Your child has a diagnosis and the general curriculum works for them with adjustments.** A 504 plan is likely the right tool.
- **Your child has a diagnosis and needs the curriculum, the instruction, or the environment changed.** An IEP is the right tool.

If MTSS interventions are not working, ask in writing for a special education evaluation. Federal law under Child Find requires schools to evaluate any child suspected of having a disability, regardless of the school's preferred tier of support.

## What if your child is in the wrong system

This is one of the most common reasons families come to us. A child placed in Tier 2 MTSS who actually needs an IEP. A child given a 504 plan when their disability requires specialized instruction. A child stuck in a holding pattern for years while the school cycles through interventions that have already failed.

If you suspect your child is in the wrong system, the family has the right to request a comprehensive evaluation in writing. The school must respond, usually within a state-specific timeline of 30 to 60 days. The evaluation answers, in writing, whether your child qualifies for an IEP, a 504 plan, or continued MTSS support.
