The school district arrives at the IEP meeting with a team. Teachers, an administrator, a school psychologist, sometimes a related-services provider. They have a folder of data, a meeting agenda, and a proposed plan. You arrive alone.

That imbalance is not an accident. It is built into the structure of the meeting itself. The good news is that you can close most of the gap before you sit down at the table. Here is what to bring.

## Documents

Bring printed copies of these items, in a labeled folder, in this order:

1. **Your child's current IEP.** Print the most recent signed version. Highlight every goal and accommodation you want to discuss.
2. **The last two progress reports.** Schools send progress reports each grading period. Bring the most recent two so you can see whether your child is meeting goals or stalling.
3. **All evaluation reports from the last three years.** Educational, psychological, occupational therapy, speech, anything that informed the current plan.
4. **Your own observation notes from the last 30 to 60 days.** Specific incidents at home, things your child has said about school, work samples you have noticed. Dated observations matter more than general impressions.
5. **A written list of three questions you must get answered before you leave the meeting.** If you write nothing else, write this.

## The three questions

These three questions, asked in this order, surface most of what families need to know:

1. **"What is my child's baseline for each goal as of today?"** If the team cannot give you a number for each goal, the goals are not measurable and the IEP needs work.
2. **"What does the data show about progress since the last meeting?"** This is where progress reports become evidence. If the data does not show progress, the supports need to change.
3. **"What is the school's plan if my child does not meet these goals by the next review?"** The answer to this question tells you whether the team has a plan at all.

If the team responds to any of those questions with vague language, slow down. Ask the team to put the answer in writing in the meeting minutes.

## Mindset

You are not at the meeting to be polite. You are at the meeting to enforce a federal civil right your child already has. Schools sometimes treat IEP meetings as collaborations, sometimes as negotiations. Approach the meeting as both. You are part of the team that makes the decision. The law explicitly says so.

A few things to do before you walk in:

- **Eat something.** Low blood sugar makes it harder to think clearly under pressure.
- **Bring a support person.** A friend, family member, advocate, or interpreter. You have the legal right to bring anyone you choose to the meeting.
- **Plan for the meeting to run long.** Block 90 minutes minimum, even for what the school calls a "quick review."

## What to do during the meeting

Take notes by hand or on a laptop. Write down every commitment the team makes, with the date attached. If the team says "we will start a new reading intervention next week," write the date that intervention starts. If the team says "we will reconvene in 30 days to review progress," write the actual calendar date 30 days out.

If you do not understand something, ask the speaker to explain it again in plain language. The right to plain-language explanation is yours. Do not nod through a technical explanation you are not following. Every term has a plain-language version.

## What to do after the meeting

Within 24 hours, send the case manager an email summarizing what you understood was agreed: "Thank you for the meeting yesterday. As I understood it, the team agreed to the following commitments." Then list each one with the date attached. Close with "Please let me know if anything in this summary differs from your notes." That email creates a paper trail you can hold the team to.

If the team's written meeting summary or amended IEP disagrees with what you understood was decided, do not sign the IEP until the document matches the conversation. You have the right to take the IEP home and review it before signing.
